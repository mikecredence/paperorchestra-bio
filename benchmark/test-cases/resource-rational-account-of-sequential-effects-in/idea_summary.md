## Working title

Resource-Rational Account of Sequential Effects in Human Prediction

## Core question

Can cognitive constraints (rather than erroneous assumptions about environmental nonstationarity) explain the sequential effects observed when humans predict stochastic stimuli?

## Motivation / gap

- Sequential effects in human prediction tasks are well documented -- people's predictions fluctuate and depart from Bayesian optimality.
- The dominant explanation says humans wrongly assume the environment is changing (nonstationarity assumption), even when it is static.
- An alternative resource-rational framework, where sub-optimal predictions arise from cognitive costs under otherwise rational behavior, has not been systematically developed or tested for this phenomenon.

## Core contribution

- Propose a costly inference framework with two model classes: (1) precision of beliefs is costly, leading to exponential forgetting, and (2) beliefs with high predictive power are favored
- Show that cognitive constraints alone can produce the same sequential effects previously attributed to nonstationarity assumptions
- Carry out behavioral experiments to compare model predictions against human data
- Provide a principled, resource-rational alternative to the nonstationarity explanation

## Method in brief

- Formal framework for costly inference with two classes of cognitive constraint models
- Model class 1: precision cost leading to exponential forgetting of past observations
- Model class 2: preference for beliefs with high predictive power
- Behavioral experiment with human participants making predictions on stochastic sequences
- Quantitative model comparison against human behavioral data
