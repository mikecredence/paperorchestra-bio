## Working title

Criticality supports cross-frequency cortical-thalamic information transfer during conscious states

## Core question

What is the nature of bidirectional cortical-thalamic communication during consciousness, and how is it disrupted during unconsciousness or altered during psychedelic states?

## Motivation / gap

- Consciousness is thought to depend on bidirectional information transfer between cortex and thalamus, but the spectral mechanisms underlying this communication are poorly understood
- It is unclear whether there is a conserved cross-frequency channel for corticothalamic communication across species
- How this communication changes during loss of consciousness (anesthesia, seizures) versus enhanced consciousness (psychedelics) remains an open question

## Core contribution

- Identify a highly preserved spectral channel: information sent via delta/theta waves (~1.5-13 Hz) from one region is encoded by high-gamma waves (~50-100 Hz) in the other
- Show this cross-frequency communication is present during conscious states across humans, mice, and rats
- Demonstrate that propofol anesthesia and generalized spike-and-wave seizures diminish this communication, while psychedelic 5-MeO-DMT enhances it
- Link this cross-frequency transfer to neural criticality, providing a mechanistic framework

## Method in brief

- Simultaneous cortical and thalamic electrophysiology (LFP/ECoG) in humans, mice, and rats
- Transfer entropy and cross-frequency coupling analysis (delta/theta to high-gamma)
- Pharmacological manipulations: propofol anesthesia (unconsciousness), 5-MeO-DMT (psychedelic state)
- Seizure model: generalized spike-and-wave events
- Criticality analysis: power-law scaling, branching ratio, avalanche statistics
