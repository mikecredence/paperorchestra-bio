## Working title

tRNA thiolation controls translation of long immune-response proteins in Arabidopsis

## Core question

How does the evolutionarily conserved tRNA thiolation modification contribute to plant immunity, and what specific translational targets does it regulate?

## Motivation / gap

- Plants activate sophisticated immune responses against pathogens, but how the translation machinery contributes to immunity is largely unknown
- tRNA thiolation (s2U34 modification) is conserved from yeast to plants and ensures efficient codon decoding during translation
- Whether tRNA thiolation specifically regulates immune-relevant transcripts, particularly long proteins enriched in thiolation-dependent codons, has not been tested in plants

## Core contribution

- Identify the cgb mutant in Arabidopsis that is hyper-susceptible to Pseudomonas syringae
- Show CGB encodes ROL5 (homolog of yeast NCS6), which physically interacts with CTU2 (homolog of yeast NCS2) to carry out tRNA thiolation
- Demonstrate that loss of ROL5 or CTU2 abolishes tRNA thiolation and compromises both transcriptional and translational immune reprogramming
- Show that translation of the salicylic acid receptor NPR1 is specifically reduced in cgb, leading to diminished SA signaling and impaired immunity

## Method in brief

- Forward genetic screen in Arabidopsis for pathogen hyper-susceptibility mutants
- Map-based cloning identifying CGB as ROL5
- Co-immunoprecipitation of ROL5 and CTU2
- tRNA thiolation assays (APM gel electrophoresis)
- RNA-seq and ribosome profiling during immune activation
- Western blot and polysome profiling for NPR1 translation
- Pathogen growth assays with Pseudomonas syringae pv. tomato DC3000
