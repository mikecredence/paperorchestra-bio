## Working title

In Vivo Photopharmacology of Opioid Receptors Using Caged Drugs for Bidirectional Manipulation

## Core question

Can we achieve precise, site-specific manipulation of endogenous opioid receptors in the living brain using systemically administered photoactivatable ("caged") opioid drugs, and can this approach reveal circuit-level adaptations during chronic opioid exposure?

## Motivation / gap

- Traditional methods for site-specific drug delivery in the brain (cannulae, microinjection) are slow, invasive, and hard to combine with neural recordings
- Photopharmacology offers spatial and temporal precision but has not been demonstrated in vivo for opioid receptor manipulation
- Understanding how mesolimbic dopamine circuits adapt during chronic morphine use requires tools with high spatiotemporal resolution that existing approaches cannot provide

## Core contribution

- Development of PhOX (photoactivatable oxymorphone, mu-opioid agonist) and PhNX (photoactivatable naloxone, mu-opioid antagonist) for bidirectional in vivo control
- Demonstration that systemic administration of caged opioids followed by focal brain illumination produces local changes in receptor occupancy, brain metabolic activity, neuronal calcium activity, neurochemical signaling, and pain/reward behaviors
- Combined PhOX photoactivation with optical dopamine recording to reveal adaptations in opioid sensitivity of mesolimbic dopamine circuitry during chronic morphine administration

## Method in brief

We will synthesize photoactivatable variants of oxymorphone (PhOX) and naloxone (PhNX) by attaching photolabile protecting groups. Drugs are administered systemically in inactive form, then activated locally in specific brain regions by targeted light delivery through implanted optical fibers. Readouts include PET-based receptor occupancy, FDG-PET metabolic imaging, fiber photometry calcium imaging, microdialysis/voltammetry for neurochemistry, and behavioral assays for pain and reward. Chronic morphine paradigm combined with PhOX and dopamine sensors will probe circuit adaptations.
