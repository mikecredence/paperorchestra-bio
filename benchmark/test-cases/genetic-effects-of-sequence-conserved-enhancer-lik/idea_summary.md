## Working title

Genetic Effects of Sequence-Conserved Enhancer-Like Elements on Human Complex Traits

## Core question

How do non-coding genomic elements that are both evolutionarily conserved (human-mouse) and biochemically active as enhancers contribute to the heritability and genetic architecture of complex human traits?

## Motivation / gap

- GWAS hits overwhelmingly land in non-coding regions, but mechanistic interpretation is still limited
- Enhancer annotations exist, and sequence conservation maps exist, but their intersection has not been systematically leveraged across a broad panel of tissues and traits
- Prior work tends to focus on one tissue or a handful of traits; nobody has done a comprehensive cross-tissue, cross-ancestry assessment

## Core contribution

- A scalable method to identify functional elements that are both sequence-conserved (human-mouse) and show enhancer-like biochemical signatures
- Application across 313 epigenomic datasets spanning 106 tissues/cell types
- Integration with 468 GWAS from European (EUR) and East Asian (EAS) cohorts to map tissue-specific heritability enrichment and causal variants
- Discovery of candidate genes for BMI and schizophrenia that were missed in prior GWAS
- Comprehensive resource linking conserved enhancers to complex trait genetics

## Method in brief

We will intersect human-mouse sequence conservation tracks with enhancer-like chromatin marks (e.g., H3K27ac, H3K4me1, DNase) across a large compendium of epigenomic datasets. The resulting "conserved enhancer-like elements" (CELEs) will be tested for heritability enrichment using stratified LD score regression (S-LDSC) against GWAS summary statistics. Fine-mapping will identify causal variants within CELEs, and gene-level analyses will nominate candidate genes for selected traits.
