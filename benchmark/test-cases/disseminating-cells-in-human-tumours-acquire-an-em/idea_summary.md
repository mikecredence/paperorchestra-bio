## Working title

EMT stem cells as predictors of metastasis: identifying disseminating cancer cells in human oral tumours

## Core question

Can we identify disseminating EMT cancer stem cells in human tumour specimens using combined epithelial/mesenchymal markers, and do these cells predict metastatic outcome?

## Motivation / gap

- Cancer stem cells (CSCs) undergoing epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) drive metastasis in experimental models, but this has not been directly observed in human tumour specimens
- Tumour cells actively disseminating into surrounding tissue have not been characterized for EMT/CSC markers in human cancers
- There is no established marker combination to identify disseminating EMT CSCs at the tumour invasive front
- The clinical predictive value of disseminating EMT CSCs for metastasis in human cancers is unknown

## Core contribution

- Identification of disseminating EpCAM+/CD24+/Vimentin+ cells at the invasive front of human oral cancers
- Significant enrichment of these triple-positive disseminating cells in metastatic vs. non-metastatic specimens
- An artificial neural network trained on these features predicts metastasis with 87-89% cross-validated accuracy
- Direct observation of EMT CSCs disseminating in human tumour tissue, bridging the gap between experimental models and clinical relevance

## Method in brief

- Multi-marker immunofluorescence staining (EpCAM, CD24, Vimentin) on 84 human oral cancer specimens
- Quantitative imaging analysis across over 12,000 fields to identify disseminating single cells beyond the tumour body
- Statistical analysis of marker co-expression enrichment in metastatic vs. non-metastatic cases
- Training an artificial neural network to predict metastasis from disseminating cell features
