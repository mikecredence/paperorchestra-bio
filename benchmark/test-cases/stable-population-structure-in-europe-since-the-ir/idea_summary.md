## Working title

Stable Population Structure in Europe Since the Iron Age, Despite High Mobility

## Core question

How did European population structure change (or not) during the historical period (3,000 YBP to present), and how do we reconcile apparent stability with evidence of high individual mobility?

## Motivation / gap

- Ancient DNA studies have shown dramatic population turnover in European prehistory (Neolithic farmer and Bronze Age Steppe ancestry waves), but little is known about changes from the Iron Age onward.
- Whole-genome data from many historical-period regions (e.g., Armenia, France) are lacking entirely.
- It is unclear whether the high mobility documented in historical records translated into significant shifts in population genetic structure.

## Core contribution

- Generate whole genomes from 204 historical-period individuals from Europe and the Mediterranean, including first-ever genomes from several regions
- Show that at least 7% of individuals carry ancestry uncommon in their sampling region, indicating cross-Mediterranean and long-distance contacts
- Demonstrate that despite this high mobility, overall population structure is remarkably stable from 3,000 YBP to the present, mirroring geography
- Reconcile high individual mobility with population-level genetic stability

## Method in brief

- Whole-genome sequencing of 204 ancient individuals from European and Mediterranean sites
- Population structure analysis (PCA, ADMIXTURE) comparing historical and modern populations
- Ancestry decomposition to identify outlier individuals with non-local ancestry
- Temporal binning to track structure changes from Iron Age through Medieval to present
- Geographic correlation analysis of genetic vs. spatial distance over time
