## Experiment 3: Population structure stability over time

Mantel test: correlation between genetic distance (Fst) and geographic distance.

| Time Period | Mantel r (genetic ~ geography) | p-value | n (populations) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Iron Age (3000-2200 YBP) | 0.78 | <0.001 | 12 |
| Roman Period (2200-1500 YBP) | 0.81 | <0.001 | 14 |
| Medieval (1500-500 YBP) | 0.80 | <0.001 | 11 |
| Modern | 0.83 | <0.001 | 15 |

Key finding: Genetic-geographic correlation is stable across all periods (r = 0.78-0.83).

## Experiment 4: Pairwise Fst between time slices within regions

| Region | Fst (Iron Age vs Modern) | Fst (Roman vs Modern) | Fst (Medieval vs Modern) |
|---|---|---|---|
| France | 0.005 | 0.004 | 0.002 |
| Italy | 0.006 | 0.004 | 0.003 |
| Iberia | 0.004 | 0.003 | 0.002 |
| Balkans | 0.007 | 0.005 | 0.003 |

All Fst values very low, indicating minimal genetic drift or replacement within regions.

## Notes

- High individual mobility (7% outliers) did not perturb overall population structure, suggesting migrants were either rare relative to census size or were absorbed without shifting allele frequencies.
- Contrast with prehistoric period: Neolithic transition Fst values were 0.03-0.08 (order of magnitude larger shifts).
- Geography has been the dominant predictor of genetic structure for the last 3,000 years.
