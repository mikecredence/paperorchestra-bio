## Working title

Integrating single-cell RNA and TCR/BCR sequencing with mass cytometry reveals dynamic trajectories of human peripheral immune cells from birth to old age

## Core question

How do human peripheral immune cell populations and their transcriptomes, immune repertoires, and cell-cell interactions change dynamically across the entire human lifespan from birth to old age?

## Motivation / gap

- Aging profoundly reshapes the immune system, but a comprehensive single-cell multi-omic atlas spanning the full human lifespan has been lacking
- Previous immune aging studies focused on limited age ranges or used bulk approaches that cannot capture cellular heterogeneity
- The dynamics of T cell and B cell receptor repertoires across the lifespan remain poorly characterized at single-cell resolution
- It is unknown which immune cell subsets undergo the most intensive rewiring at specific life stages

## Core contribution

- Profile peripheral immune cells from 220 healthy volunteers spanning 13 age groups (0 to >90 years) from the Shanghai Pudong Cohort
- Integrate single-cell RNA-seq with TCR/BCR sequencing, high-throughput mass cytometry, bulk RNA-seq, and flow cytometry validation
- Reveal that T cells are the most strongly affected by age, with different subsets displaying distinct aging patterns in both transcriptomes and immune repertoires
- Identify GNLY+CD8+ effector memory T cells as the subset with the highest clonal expansion
- Discover a population of cytotoxic B cells enriched in children
- Present a single-cell immune aging clock to assess individual immune status across life stages

## Method in brief

220 healthy volunteers from the Shanghai Pudong Cohort spanning 13 age groups (0 to >90 years) were profiled using single-cell RNA-sequencing coupled with single T cell and B cell receptor sequencing, high-throughput mass cytometry (CyTOF), bulk RNA-sequencing, and flow cytometry validation experiments. Computational analyses characterized dynamic trajectories, clonal expansion patterns, cell-cell interactions, and age-associated transcriptomic changes across immune cell types.
