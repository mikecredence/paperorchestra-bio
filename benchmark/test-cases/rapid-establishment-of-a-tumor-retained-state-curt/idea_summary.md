## Working title

Rapid Establishment of a Tumor-Retained State Curtails the Contribution of Conventional NK Cells to Anti-Tumor Immunity in Solid Cancers

## Core question

What happens to conventional NK cells after they enter a solid tumor, and how quickly do they lose effector function?

## Motivation / gap

- NK cells are important for early tumor control and metastasis prevention, but established tumors contain a phenotypically distinct NK cell population with unclear origins and dynamics.
- The temporal kinetics of NK cell dysfunction in tumors were not well characterized -- we did not know how fast NK cells lose function after entering the tumor microenvironment.
- Whether the tumor-resident NK pool actually contributes to anti-tumor immunity in established cancers was an open question.

## Core contribution

- Demonstrate using photo-labeling and longitudinal transcriptomic analysis that conventional NK cells are continuously recruited into tumors but rapidly adopt a tissue-resident-like, non-functional state within 48-72 hours of tumor entry
- Show that this transition involves complete loss of effector functions including chemokine/cytokine production and cytotoxicity
- Reveal that depletion of NK cells from established tumors does not alter tumor growth, indicating the tumor-retained NK cells are functionally inert
- Validated across multiple murine cancer models

## Method in brief

- Photo-labeling approach to track NK cell fate after tumor entry in vivo
- Longitudinal transcriptomic profiling (scRNA-seq) of tumor-infiltrating NK cells
- Cellular phenotypic analysis by flow cytometry at multiple timepoints
- NK cell depletion in established tumors to test functional contribution
- Multiple murine solid tumor models for generalizability
