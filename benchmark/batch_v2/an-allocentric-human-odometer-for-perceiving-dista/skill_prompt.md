## Idea Summary

# Idea Summary: Allocentric Human Odometer for Distance Perception on the Ground Plane

## Working title
An allocentric human odometer for perceiving distances on the ground plane

## Core question
Does the visual system use an allocentric (world-centered) or egocentric (body-centered) spatial reference frame for coding visual space during self-motion, and what are the properties of the path-integration mechanism that maintains this reference?

## Motivation / gap
- We reliably judge object locations despite constant retinal image motion during walking, but the coordinate system underlying this stability was unknown
- Prior studies only tested stationary observers, making it impossible to distinguish allocentric from egocentric coding (both predict the same result without self-motion)
- The intrinsic bias (an implicit curved surface representation used to localize objects in the dark) was known, but whether it moves with the observer or stays anchored to the ground was untested
- The role of vestibular vs. proprioceptive signals in path-integration for visual space perception was unclear
- Whether path-integration operates isotropically (equally in all directions) or anisotropically was unknown

## Core contribution (bullet form)
- Demonstrated that the intrinsic bias remains anchored at the home base (allocentric coding) rather than traveling with the observer during forward walking (Experiment 1, n=8, 12-sec delay condition)
- Showed that the allocentric reference decays over time: the shift between walking and baseline conditions was smaller with a 60-sec delay compared to a 12-sec delay
- Proved that dividing attention during walking disrupts path-integration, causing the intrinsic bias to travel with the observer (Experiment 2, n=8)
- Established that passive vestibular translation alone is sufficient for path-integration: forward wheelchair transport produced similar results to active walking (Experiment 3, n=8)
- Demonstrated anisotropic path-integration: the mechanism operates along the horizontal direction only, not vertically (Experiment 5, stepladder descent, n=9)
- Found backward passive translation shifted the intrinsic bias in the opposite direction (Experiment 4, n=8), confirming directional sensitivity of the odometer

## Method in brief
Five experiments were conducted in a dark room with unknown layout. Observers (14 total for Experiments 1-4, 8 per experiment; 9 for Experiment 5) judged the location of a briefly presented (1 sec, 5 Hz flicker) dimly lit green LED target (0.16 cd/m2, 0.22 deg visual angle) using a blind walking-gesturing task: after viewing the target, they walked blindly along a guidance rope to the remembered location and gestured its height. Music masked auditory cues.

In the baseline-stationary condition, observers stood at the home base and judged target location. In walking conditions, observers walked blindly 1.5 m from the home base before target presentation (with 12 or 60 sec delays). The allocentric hypothesis predicts that the intrinsic bias stays at the home base, so targets should appear nearer after forward walking. The egocentric hypothesis predicts the bias moves with the observer, so targets appear at the same location. Additional experiments tested divided attention during walking, passive vestibular-only transport (wheelchair), backward translation, and vertical descent (stepladder) to characterize the path-integration mechanism.

Experiment 5 used a stepladder descent paradigm to test whether the odometer gauges only horizontal distance. Observers stood on a stepladder (0.6 m height), descended in the dark, and then judged target locations. If path-integration only operates horizontally, the intrinsic bias's horizontal coordinate should remain at the home base while the vertical coordinate shifts downward.

## Experiment 1: Allocentric vs. Egocentric Hypothesis

### Conditions

| Condition | Description | Delay |
|---|---|---|
| Baseline-stationary | Observer stands at home base, judges target | 12 or 60 sec |
| Walking (12 sec delay) | Walk 1.5 m, wait 12 sec, judge target | 12 sec |
| Walking (60 sec delay) | Walk 1.5 m, wait 60 sec, judge target | 60 sec |

### Key Results

| Comparison | Finding | Direction | Significance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Walking (12 sec) vs Baseline-stationary | Judged locations significantly nearer in walking | Supports allocentric | Significant |
| Walking (60 sec) vs Baseline-stationary | Much smaller separation than 12 sec | Allocentric effect decays | Reduced |
| Intrinsic bias shift | ~1.35 m horizontal shift | Consistent with 1.5 m walk | Estimated from Fig. 3c |

- Fig. 3c: Average judged target locations (n=8) plotted for all conditions
- Filled green triangles (12 sec walking) are significantly nearer than red circles (baseline-stationary)
- Open green triangles (60 sec walking) show smaller separation from baseline
- Blue and gray intrinsic bias curves fitted by eye show ~1.35 m horizontal shift
- Black circle on vertical axis represents average observer eye height
- Fig. 3e-f: Angular declination plots showing judged vs physical angles

## Experiment 5: Stepladder Descent (Anisotropic Path-Integration)

### Design Parameters

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Stepladder height | 0.6 m |
| Participants | n=9 |
| Manipulation | Observer descends from stepladder in the dark |

### Conditions

| Condition | Description |
|---|---|
| Stepladder | Observer descends 0.6 m vertically, judges target from floor |
| Ground-level baseline | Observer stands on floor, judges target |

### Predictions

| Hypothesis | Horizontal Coordinate | Vertical Coordinate |
|---|---|---|
| Isotropic path-integration | Stays at home base | Stays at stepladder height |
| Horizontal-only path-integration | Stays at home base (floor location) | Shifts down with observer |

### Key Results

| Comparison | Horizontal | Vertical | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stepladder vs Ground-level | No horizontal shift | Vertical shift observed | Horizontal-only path-integration |
| Implication | Odometer only gauges horizontal distance | Vertical motion not integrated | Anisotropic |

- Fig. 5a: Prediction diagram for horizontal-only path-integration
- Fig. 5c: Average results (n=9) showing anisotropic pattern
- The path-integration mechanism operates exclusively along the horizontal direction
- This finding parallels anisotropic spatial memory observed in desert ants

## Intrinsic Bias Properties

| Property | Finding |
|---|---|
| Shape | Curved surface representation |
| Default behavior | Used to localize objects in the dark |
| Integration | Combines with visible depth cues (e.g., texture gradients) |
| Coordinate system | Allocentric (world-centered) |
| Anchoring | Fixed at home base location on ground |
| Path-integration | Updates with observer motion (vestibular signals) |
| Attention dependency | Requires attentional resources during locomotion |
| Temporal decay | Effect diminishes from 12 sec to 60 sec post-walking |
| Directionality | Encodes direction of travel (forward vs backward) |
| Anisotropy | Horizontal motion only (no vertical integration) |
