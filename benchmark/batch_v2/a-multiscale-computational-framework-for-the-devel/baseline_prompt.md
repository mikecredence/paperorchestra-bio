# Idea Summary

## Working title
A multiscale computational framework for the development of spines in molluscan shells

## Core question
Can we build a modular computational framework that links molecular-scale biochemical patterning to tissue-level mechanical growth to explain the diversity of spine shapes in the mollusc Turbo sazae, and can this framework detect population-level differences in spine morphology across different collection sites?

## Motivation / gap
- Connecting genotype to phenotype across spatial scales remains a fundamental challenge in developmental biology, especially for non-model organisms
- Molluscan shells provide a unique record of developmental processes, but existing geometric models of shell form are disconnected from underlying biological mechanisms
- Mathematical models of shell growth and pigmentation exist at various scales, but attempts to combine them across scales have been rare due to the difficulty of integrating categorically different modeling approaches
- Spine-forming molluscs like T. sazae are not model organisms, so gene regulatory networks are unknown, making direct molecular-to-phenotype mapping infeasible
- T. sazae exhibits remarkable phenotypic plasticity in spine form across populations, but no prior study has quantified this variation or proposed a mechanistic explanation

## Core contribution (bullet form)
- Developed a modular multiscale framework linking a two-species reaction-diffusion biochemical model (Activator-Substrate) to a tissue-level elastic rod mechanical model for spine growth
- Used a Quality-Diversity (MAP-Elites) algorithm to exhaustively explore the biochemical parameter space and build a look-up table mapping 6 molecular parameters to growth profile parameters {g1, g2}
- Demonstrated that the growth parameters g1 (growth rate) and g2 (growth width) control spine curvature and width respectively, producing a morphospace of realistic spine shapes
- Applied the framework to real shell specimens from three populations in Japan (Hayama, Jyogashima, Tateyama) and detected a statistically significant difference in spine form between Jyogashima and the other two sites
- Traced the population-level morphological difference through the model: Jyogashima specimens had larger g1 values, corresponding to larger substrate production rate c2 and lower activator degradation rate c3 at the molecular level
- The framework is modular, allowing different molecular or mechanical models to be swapped without changing the overall methodology

## Method in brief
The framework has four components. First, a biochemical model uses a two-species Activator-Substrate reaction-diffusion system (adapted from Fowler et al.) governed by six parameters S = {c1, c2, c3, c4, d1, d2}. This produces spatially inhomogeneous concentration profiles. Second, a tissue-level mechanical model treats the mantle edge as an elastic rod undergoing incremental growth defined by a Gaussian growth profile g(S0) parameterized by g1 (growth rate amplitude) and g2 (growth width). The mechanical model simulates the spine shape emerging from this growth process over a time sequence.

Third, spine images from real shells are matched against the library of simulated spine shapes to find the best-fit {g1, g2} pair. Fourth, the biochemical parameters producing the matched growth profile are retrieved from the look-up table generated by a Quality-Diversity algorithm (MAP-Elites). The MAP-Elites approach explores the biochemical parameter space to find the full diversity of achievable {g1, g2} pairs, storing multiple parameter sets per growth profile. These steps (biochemical exploration and mechanical simulation) are performed once as precomputation and then function as look-up tables for rapid matching of real specimens.

The framework was applied to spines from T. sazae shells collected from three localities around Tokyo Bay in Japan: Hayama, Jyogashima, and Tateyama. Population-level differences were assessed by comparing the distributions of best-fit {g1, g2} values and their corresponding molecular parameters across the three sites.

## Tissue-Level Growth Model

The incremental growth profile at the tissue level follows a Gaussian form:

g(S0) is parameterized by:
- g1: amplitude of the growth rate (arc length increase per time for a given material section)
- g2: width of the growing region

The total stretch lambda relates the current arc length parameter s to the initial parameter S0 and growth parameter S via: lambda = (ds/dS)(dS/dS0). The variable l is the total current length of the rod.

## Experiment 1: Morphospace of Spine Shapes (Growth Parameter Variation)

Fig. 4a shows the morphospace as g1 and g2 are varied.

| g1 Effect | g2 Effect | Spine Morphology |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Larger g1 | Fixed g2 | More curved spine (pronounced mantle deformation per time step) |
| Smaller g1 | Fixed g2 | Straighter, taller spine before self-contact |
| Fixed g1 | Larger g2 | Wider spine (wider growing region) |
| Fixed g1 | Smaller g2 | Narrower spine tip (narrower growing region + decreased stiffness) |
| Low g1, low g2 | Both low | Narrow, straight spine |
| High g1, high g2 | Both high | Wide, strongly curved spine |

Key observations from Fig. 4a:
- Increasing g1 produces more curved spines because the excess length per time step increases mantle deformation curvature
- Decreasing g1 leads to slower mantle evolution, producing straighter shapes closer to the shell edge
- Increasing g2 widens the spine and the tip
- The combination of low g2 with any g1 yields notably narrow spine tips

## Limitations and Notes

| Limitation | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Phenomenological biochemical model | Not based on a real gene regulatory network for T. sazae |
| Two-species simplification | Real biochemistry likely involves more interacting species |
| No direct environmental-molecular link | Cannot yet formally connect environmental factors to specific biochemical parameters |
| Not all specimens grow spines | The framework addresses spine shape but not the binary presence/absence decision |
| Static framework | Models are run independently at each scale rather than as a fully coupled multiscale simulation |
