## Idea Summary

## Working title

Separating local and volume-conducted contributions to thalamic local field potentials using kernel current source density analysis in the rat somatosensory system

## Core question

How much of the evoked potential recorded in the rat thalamus during whisker stimulation is genuinely generated by local thalamic currents versus passively volume-conducted from the large-amplitude cortical response, and can kernel CSD (kCSD) analysis reliably recover the truly local thalamic signal?

## Motivation / gap

- Local field potentials (LFP) are widely used for population-level neural monitoring, but volume conduction causes distant high-amplitude sources to contaminate recordings at remote sites, and there is no consensus on the spatial extent of this spread (estimates range from 250 um to several millimeters)
- The rat whisker-barrel system is an important model for thalamocortical processing, but the barrel cortex generates very large evoked potentials (~10 ms post-stimulus) that can passively propagate into subcortical structures including the thalamus
- Thalamic nuclei are composed of stellate neurons forming nearly closed electric fields, producing small but measurable evoked potentials; the question of how much of these signals is local vs. remote has not been systematically addressed
- Standard CSD analysis can recover local currents from LFP but has not been applied to the specific problem of thalamic signal decontamination from cortical volume conduction in multi-structure recordings
- It is unknown whether concurrent cortical recordings are actually necessary for reliable thalamic CSD estimation, which has practical implications for experimental design

## Core contribution (bullet form)

- Demonstrated that the negative LFP wave recorded in thalamic nuclei around 7-15 ms post-whisker-stimulus has a substantial component passively volume-conducted from cortex, with thalamic-cortical EP correlation above 0.5 at 10-12 ms post-stimulus (n=11 rats)
- Used kernel CSD (kCSD) method to analytically separate local thalamic currents from cortical field contributions, recovering truly local thalamic signals
- Showed that thalamic EP reconstructed from local thalamic sources alone is significantly less negative at 10 ms post-stimulus than the raw recorded EP (permutation paired-test, p < 0.05), confirming cortical contamination
- Demonstrated across three experimental setups (Neuropixels 384-channel, NeuroNexus 8x8, NeuroNexus 32-channel) that the framework generalizes across probe technologies
- Critically, showed that concurrent cortical recordings are NOT essential for reliable thalamic CSD estimation -- thalamic-only recordings with sources assumed only in the thalamus yield similar CSD profiles
- Quantified the spatial reach of cortical field potentials, showing the strong negative cortical field at 10 ms extends as a continuous stripe from cortex through the entire subcortical space to the thalamus

## Method in brief

Eleven adult male Wistar rats (350-560 g) were anesthetized with urethane (1.5 mg/kg i.p.) and instrumented for simultaneous recordings from the barrel cortex and somatosensory thalamus (VPM and PoM nuclei). Whisker stimulation was delivered via a piezoelectric stimulator producing 0.1 mm horizontal deflections (1-2 ms duration, 20V, 100 stimuli at 3-5 s pseudorandom intervals). Three recording configurations were used: (1) Neuropixels 1.0 probes with 384 channels spanning from cortex through thalamus, (2) two NeuroNexus 8x8 multi-shank silicon probes (one in cortex, one in thalamus), and (3) dual 32-channel NeuroNexus linear probes. Cortical probes were tilted ~30 degrees from vertical for perpendicular cortical entry; thalamic probes were inserted vertically.

The kernel CSD (kCSD) method was applied to the averaged evoked potential profiles to estimate current source density in the recording volume. The key analysis involved computing CSD from the full cortico-thalamic profile, then selectively reconstructing field potentials from only cortical sources or only thalamic sources to quantify each structure's contribution to the recorded signal at any point. This source decomposition was performed under several configurations: (A) thalamic + cortical recordings with sources assumed only in thalamus, (B) thalamic recordings alone with sources assumed only in thalamus, and (C) full cortico-thalamic recordings with sources spanning the entire tissue block. Measured thalamic EPs were compared to reconstructed local-source-only EPs at 10 ms post-stimulus, and the difference was tested statistically using permutation paired tests. Electrode positions were verified histologically with cytochrome oxidase and Nissl staining.

## 14. Implications for LFP Interpretation

| Issue | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Thalamic LFP contains cortical contamination | Apply CSD analysis before interpreting thalamic signals |
| Volume conduction from barrel cortex | Can affect subcortical recordings including olfactory bulb (per prior literature) |
| Hippocampal oscillations | May also spread widely; similar CSD approach recommended |
| Human intracranial recordings | Same volume conduction concerns apply |
| Experimental design | Concurrent cortical recording is helpful but not required for thalamic CSD |
