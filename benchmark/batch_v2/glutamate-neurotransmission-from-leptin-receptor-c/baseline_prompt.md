# Idea Summary

## Working title
Glutamate Neurotransmission from Leptin Receptor Cells Is Required for Typical Puberty and Reproductive Function in Female Mice

## Core question
Is glutamatergic signaling from leptin-responsive ventral premammillary nucleus (PMv) neurons required for leptin's control of puberty onset and reproductive function in female mice?

## Motivation / gap
- Leptin is an essential metabolic signal for the reproductive axis, but the neural circuitry and neurotransmitter mechanisms involved are incompletely understood
- The PMv is a glutamatergic nucleus densely expressing leptin receptor (LepRb) that projects directly to GnRH neurons, yet its role has been often overlooked
- Previous conditional deletion studies showed that removing LepRb from all Vglut2-expressing neurons causes virtually no reproductive deficits, while deleting LepRb from GABAergic neurons causes large deficits
- This led to the prevailing conclusion that GABAergic, not glutamatergic, neurotransmission mediates leptin's reproductive effects
- However, the global Vglut2-neuron deletion is too broad and may mask the specific contribution of PMv glutamate signaling through compensatory mechanisms in other populations
- No study had directly tested whether acute activation of LepRb PMv neurons induces LH secretion in a fed state, or whether glutamate is specifically required for PMv-mediated pubertal development

## Core contribution (bullet form)
- Demonstrated that chemogenetic (DREADD) activation of LepRb neurons in the PMv induces transient LH release in fed adult females, with 15 of 18 mice showing successful PMv targeting; LH levels correlated with the number of cFos-immunoreactive PMv neurons
- Showed that LepRb-deltaVglut2 females (conditional Vglut2 deletion in LepRb neurons) have delayed age of first estrus (p = 0.028), disrupted estrous cycles, increased GnRH content in median eminence terminals (p = 0.0005), and impaired LH responses
- Demonstrated in a PMv-specific rescue model (LepRloxTB with stereotaxic AAV-Cre) that restoring LepRb in the PMv rescues vaginal opening, follicle maturation, and pregnancy, while concomitant Vglut2 deletion (LepRloxTB;Vglut2flox + AAV-Cre) abolishes pubertal development entirely
- Clozapine (CNO metabolite) also induced LH release in hM3Dq-expressing animals (n=4), confirming DREADD-mediated activation
- PMv-miss animals and AAV-mCherry negative controls showed no LH increase, validating spatial specificity

## Method in brief
Three main experimental approaches were used. First, chemogenetic activation: LepRb-Cre mice received unilateral stereotaxic injections of AAV-hM3Dq into the PMv. After 4 weeks, mice were cannulated for serial blood sampling via the Culex Automated Blood Sampling System. Baseline LH was measured at 3 time points (every 10 min), followed by intravenous CNO (0.5 mg/kg) or clozapine injection, and LH was sampled for 1 hour afterward. PMv-hits vs. PMv-misses were classified based on mCherry and cFos immunoreactivity post-hoc.

Second, conditional knockout: LepRb-deltaVglut2 mice were generated by crossing LepRb-Cre with Vglut2-floxed mice. Reproductive phenotyping included age of vaginal opening, age of first estrus, estrous cyclicity monitoring, basal LH levels, LH surge assessment, and GnRH immunoreactivity quantification in the arcuate nucleus and median eminence by fluorescence microscopy.

Third, PMv-specific rescue with concomitant Vglut2 deletion: LepRloxTB (LepR-null, Cre-inducible rescue) mice were crossed with Vglut2-floxed mice. Stereotaxic injection of AAV-Cre into the PMv simultaneously restored LepRb expression and deleted Vglut2 locally. Controls received AAV-Cre to restore LepRb alone (without Vglut2 flox). Pubertal development, ovarian histology, and fertility (breeding tests) were assessed.

## Overall Conclusions from Data

1. Acute unilateral activation of LepRb PMv neurons induces transient LH secretion in fed mice, with LH levels correlating to the extent of neuronal activation.
2. Conditional deletion of Vglut2 in LepRb neurons delays puberty (first estrus), disrupts estrous cycles, increases GnRH accumulation in terminals (suggesting impaired release), and alters LH dynamics.
3. In a PMv-specific rescue model, restoring LepRb alone in the PMv rescues full reproductive function, but simultaneous deletion of Vglut2 in the PMv completely prevents pubertal development.
4. These results establish that glutamatergic neurotransmission from LepRb PMv neurons is required for leptin's control of the reproductive axis, challenging the previous conclusion that only GABAergic leptin signaling matters for reproduction.
5. The discrepancy with earlier global Vglut2-LepRb deletion studies likely reflects compensatory mechanisms or the dilution effect of deleting Vglut2 across all LepRb-expressing glutamatergic neurons.
