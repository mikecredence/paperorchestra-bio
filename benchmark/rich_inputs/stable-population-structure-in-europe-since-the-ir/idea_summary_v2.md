## Working title
Stable Population Structure in Europe Since the Iron Age Despite High Individual Mobility

## Core question
How did population genetic structure across western Eurasia change from the Iron Age to the present, and how can high individual mobility coexist with remarkably stable overall population structure?

## Motivation / gap
- Ancient DNA has revealed dramatic prehistoric population changes (Neolithic farmer expansion, Steppe migrations), but little is known about historical period (last 3,000 years) dynamics
- Individual regional studies (Rome, Iberia) show high heterogeneity and mobility, but no comprehensive multi-region assessment exists
- First historical-period genomes from several regions (Armenia, Algeria, Austria, France) had never been generated
- Standard population genetics models predict that observed mobility levels should collapse population structure, creating a paradox
- The relationship between individual-level dispersal and population-level structure in historical periods is not understood

## Core contribution (bullet form)
- Generated 204 new whole genomes from 53 archaeological sites in 18 countries spanning the historical period, including first genomes from Armenia, Algeria, Austria, and France
- Found at least 7% of historical individuals carry ancestry uncommon in their sampling region, indicating extensive cross-Mediterranean contacts
- Demonstrated that overall population structure remains geographically stable from the Iron Age through the present despite high individual mobility
- Showed via simulation that observed dispersal levels under standard panmictic models would collapse structure, implying lower effective migration than apparent dispersal
- Proposed transient dispersal hypothesis: improved transportation networks and Roman Empire mobilization moved people without proportional gene flow

## Method in brief
Whole genome sequencing was performed on 204 individuals (median depth 0.92x, range 0.16-2.38x) from historical-period archaeological sites. DNA was extracted primarily from petrous bones, with libraries treated with partial UDG. Pseudohaploid genotypes were called on the 1240k SNP panel (median 685,058 SNPs per sample). Radiocarbon dating was performed on 126 samples. Genomes were analyzed alongside 2,033 present-day genomes, 1,998 prehistoric genomes, and 764 published historical genomes.

Population structure was assessed using PCA projection onto present-day European genetic variation, qpAdm modeling for ancestry decomposition, and clustering to identify genetic outliers. Outlier individuals (those with ancestry uncommon in their region) were identified and their potential sources traced via one-component qpAdm modeling. Stepping-stone migration simulations tested whether observed mobility levels could maintain the observed stability of population structure under standard population genetics assumptions.
