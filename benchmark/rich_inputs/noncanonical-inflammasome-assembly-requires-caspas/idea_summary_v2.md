# Idea Summary

## Working title
Caspase-11 catalytic activity and autoprocessing are required for noncanonical inflammasome speck formation

## Core question
Does caspase-11 (Casp11) form visible oligomeric specks (SMOCs) in response to cytosolic LPS, and if so, what molecular determinants -- catalytic activity, autoprocessing at the interdomain linker -- are required for this higher-order assembly?

## Motivation / gap
- Casp11 is known to bind LPS through its CARD domain and oligomerize into a high-molecular-weight noncanonical inflammasome, but no one has directly visualized Casp11 SMOC formation (speck assembly) in living macrophages
- The relationship between Casp11 enzymatic activity and its oligomerization is unclear; the field assumes CARD-LPS binding alone drives assembly
- Previous work showed that both catalytic activity (C254) and IDL cleavage (D285) are needed for GSDMD processing, but whether these steps also control the upstream assembly of the inflammasome complex itself is unknown
- Dimerization alone is insufficient for full Casp11 activation, suggesting oligomerization may be a separately regulated step
- It is not resolved whether Casp11 autoprocessing occurs in cis (intra-molecular) or trans (inter-molecular), which has implications for the mechanism of inflammasome nucleation

## Core contribution (bullet form)
- Developed a Casp11-mCherry fluorescent reporter and demonstrated that LPS transfection induces dose-dependent perinuclear speck formation in Casp11-/- BMDMs reconstituted with wild-type Casp11-mCherry
- Showed that the catalytically dead C254A mutant completely fails to form specks in macrophages despite comparable expression levels, proving catalytic activity is essential for SMOC assembly
- Demonstrated in HEK293T cells that both catalytic activity (C254) and IDL cleavage at D285 are independently required for speck formation -- the D285A non-cleavable mutant does not form specks
- Engineered a TEV-cleavable Casp11 construct to show that exogenous protease cleavage at the IDL can rescue speck formation in catalytically dead Casp11, confirming that physical cleavage (not just catalytic activity per se) is the key step
- Used co-transfection of untagged WT Casp11 with tagged C254A-mCherry to show that wild-type Casp11 rescues C254A speck formation, but this rescue requires cis-autoprocessing of C254A itself (the C254A/D285A double mutant is not rescued)
- Proposed a revised model: LPS binding triggers initial Casp11 dimerization, dimerization activates catalytic activity, catalytic activity mediates intra-molecular autoprocessing at D285, and the processed form then assembles into the higher-order oligomeric SMOC

## Method in brief
The study uses a Casp11-mCherry fusion reporter expressed via lentiviral transduction in Casp11-/- bone marrow-derived macrophages (BMDMs) or via transient transfection in HEK293T cells. Key mutants include C254A (catalytically dead), D285A (non-cleavable at the IDL), and the C254A/D285A double mutant. Speck formation is quantified by confocal fluorescence microscopy as the percentage of mCherry-positive cells containing at least one perinuclear speck. GSDMD cleavage and Casp11 processing are monitored by western blot, and cytotoxicity is measured by LDH release.

To dissect whether physical cleavage (rather than catalytic activity) drives oligomerization, a TEV protease cleavage site (ENLYFQ/G) was inserted at the IDL of the D285A mutant. Co-expression with TEV protease allows exogenous cleavage of Casp11 independent of its own catalytic machinery. Co-transfection experiments with varying doses of untagged WT or mutant Casp11 alongside fixed amounts of mCherry-tagged reporters further distinguish cis versus trans processing contributions to speck formation.
